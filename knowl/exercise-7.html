Exercise 7.

Let \(R\) be a ring. Show that if \(0\neq r\in R\) has no left inverse but has a right inverse then \(r\) has infinitely many right inverses.
We show that if \(n>0\) then \(r\) has at least \(n+1\) right inverses.
  1. Assume that there exists \(n>0\) such that \(s_k\) is a right inverse of \(r\) for \(0\leq k\leq n\text{,}\) and \(s_k\neq s_\ell\) for \(k\neq\ell\text{.}\)
  2. For each \(k\in\{0,1,\ldots,n-1\}\) we put
    \begin{equation*} t_k=s_0+1-s_kr. \end{equation*}
    Show that \(rt_k=1\text{,}\) i.e., each \(t_k\) is a right inverse of \(r\text{.}\)
  3. Show that \(t_k\neq t_\ell\) for \(k\neq\ell\) and \(k,\ell\in\{0,1,\ldots,n-1\}\text{.}\)
Conclude the proof.
Give an example of a ring and a nonzero element with no left inverse but infinitely many right inverses.
