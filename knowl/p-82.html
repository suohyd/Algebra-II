Consider the map \(\varphi\colon A\to A/\mfa_1\times\cdots\times A/\mfa_n\) given by
\begin{equation*} a\mapsto(a+\mfa_1,\ldots,a+\mfa_n) \end{equation*}
It is left to the reader to check that \(\varphi\) is a well-defined ring homomorphism. We compute its kernel.
\begin{align*} \ker(\varphi)\amp=\{a\in A:a\in\mfa_i\;\text{for all}\; i\}\\ \amp=\mfa_1\cap\cdots\cap\mfa_n \end{align*}
We now show that \(\varphi\) is surjective. It is enough to show that \((1+\mfa_1,0+\mfa_2,\ldots,0+\mfa_n)\) is in the image of \(\varphi\text{.}\) Since \(\mfa_1+\mfa_i=A\) for each \(i\geq 2\) there are \(x_i\in\mfa_1\) and \(y_i\in\mfa_i\) such that \(x_i+y_i=1\) for each \(i\geq 2\text{.}\) We put \(y=y_2y_3\cdots y_n\text{.}\) Then
\begin{equation*} y\equiv(1-x_2)(1-x_3)\cdots(1-x_n)\equiv 1\mod\mfa_1 \end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*} y\equiv 0\mod\mfa_i\quad\text{for}\,i\geq 2. \end{equation*}
Hence \(\varphi(y)=(1+\mfa_1,0+\mfa_2,\cdots,0+\mfa_n)\text{.}\) By TheoremÂ 3.2.1, we get the required result.
