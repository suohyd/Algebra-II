Example 4.4.12. (Radical of \((Y^2-X^3)\)).

The below example has connections to geometry. We will not make the connection explicit in this course. However, you may see the curve \(Y^2-X^3\) in \(\R^2\) here 1 .
Consider \(\Q[X,Y]\) and the ideal generated by \(Y^2-X^3\in\Q[X,Y]\text{,}\) \((Y^2-X^3)\text{.}\) We show that if \(f(X,Y)\in\Q[X,Y]\) is such that \(f(a^2,a^3)\) for any \(a\in\Q\) then \(f(X,Y)\in(Y^2-X^3)\text{.}\) We consider \(f(X,Y),Y^2-X^3\) as polynomials over \(\Q[X]\) in variable \(Y\text{.}\) Thus we can apply Corollary 4.4.2 to get the following.
\begin{equation*} f=q(X,Y)(Y^2-X^3)+r(X)Y+s(X),\quad\text{where}\quad r(X),s(X)\in\Q[X] \end{equation*}
By the assumption, \(f(a^2,a^3)=0\) for every \(a\in\Q\text{,}\) and hence we get that
\begin{equation*} r(a^2)a^3+s(a^2)=0\quad\text{for every}\quad a\in\Q. \end{equation*}
This shows that the polynomial \(r(X^2)X^3+s(X^2)\in\Q[X]\) has infinitely many roots in \(\Q\text{.}\) By Corollary 4.4.9, we must have \(r(X^2)X^3+s(X^2)=0\in\Q[X]\text{.}\) Comparing even degree and odd degree terms we get that \(r=s=0\in\Q[X]\text{.}\) Thus, \(f\in(Y^2-X^3)\) and the resuLC is proved.
