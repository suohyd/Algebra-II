Proposition 1.1.6.

Let \(R\) be a ring with unity. We have the following properties.
  1. \(\displaystyle \left(\sum_{i=1}^{m}r_i\right)\cdot\left(\sum_{j=1}^{n}s_j\right)=\sum_{i,j}(r_i\cdot s_j)\)
  2. For any \(r,s\in R\) we have
    \begin{equation*} 0\cdot r=r\cdot 0=0\quad\text{and}\quad (-r)\cdot s=-(rs)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(-r\) and \(-(rs)\) are the additive inverses of \(r\) and \(rs\text{,}\) respectively. In particular, the additive inverse of \(r\in R\) is \(-1\cdot r\text{.}\)
  3. If \(r\in R\) commutes with \(s\in R\text{,}\) i.e., if \(r\cdot s=s\cdot r\) then we have the binomial theorem.
    \begin{equation*} (r+s)^n=r^n+{n\choose 1} r^{n-1}\cdot s+\cdots+s^n \end{equation*}
